The Profits of Power

10th Grade Industrial Revolution Project

Essential Question:

How did private companies (like Krupp or Vickers) profit from the constant need for newer, deadlier steel weapons, and how does this mirror today's tech-military relationship?

Source 1: The "Cannon King" of Germany

Source: Britannica: Alfred Krupp and the House of Krupp

"As a result... of the performance of Krupp guns in the Franco-German War of 1870–71, the firm came to be called ‘the Arsenal of the Reich.’ Alfred Krupp was in many ways the founder of modern warfare. At the time of his death he had armed 46 nations."
Main Point: Global Standardization

Krupp used military victories as marketing. Once one nation bought steel cannons, every neighbor felt forced to buy them to avoid being obsolete.

Connection to Topic: This shows how private profit was driven by an international arms race. Profit came from making sure everyone felt they needed the newest steel to survive.

Source 2: Vickers & Krupp (Profiting from All Sides)

Source: Arming All Sides: Vickers and Krupp

"German troops were torn to shreds by shells with fuzes stamped 'Krupp Patent Zünder' [Krupp Patent Fuzes] throughout the First World War. Vickers... and Krupp traded in weapons from 1902... Vickers cut deals allowing them to use the Krupp blueprints."
Main Point: Neutrality for Profit

Companies like Vickers and Krupp operated above national borders, trading patents and blueprints so they could profit regardless of which country was winning.

Connection to Topic: Highlights the "Merchants of Death" concept. These private firms profited from the volume of global conflict, effectively selling the tools of war to both allies and enemies.

Source 3: Modern Tech & The Digital Arms Race

Source: Intereconomics: Big Tech & The Military Complex

"Big Tech companies are now key players in the clash... [they] control tools (among them, cloud systems or AI algorithms aimed at... military targeting) that are essential for surveilling adversaries."
Main Point: The Shift to Software

The modern military "edge" is no longer just iron; it is data. Militaries are now fully dependent on private tech companies for AI and cloud infrastructure.

Connection to Topic: This mirrors the 19th-century reliance on private steel. If a private company owns the most advanced AI, the government is forced to pay any price to keep up with rivals.

Modern Connection Summary

The relationship between 19th-century steel giants and 21st-century tech giants is almost identical. While the material has changed from heavy steel to digital code, the business model remains the same: private companies innovate faster than governments, creating a "must-have" technology that fuels a constant, profitable arms race.
